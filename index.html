About Formula 1

Formula 1, also known as F1 and officially called the FIA Formula One World Championship, is the highest class of single-seater racing approved by the International Automobile Federation. The term "Formula" refers to the set of rules that must be followed. The Formula 1 season consists of a series of races known as Grand Prix, which are held on purpose-built circuits or public roads. The results of each race are combined with a points system that determines two annual World Championships, one for drivers and one for constructors. Europe has traditionally been the center of Formula 1 and remains its leading market, although Grand Prix are held around the world and their number is increasing. As the most expensive sport in the world, Formula 1's economic impact is also significant, and its political and financial upheavals can be observed. Formula 1 has its roots in motor racing on European soil in the 1920s and 1930s. Its sporting name, Formula 1, denotes the competitor in a race who is the fastest and has the highest competitive spirit. Regarding the technological side, the future is expected to be quite revolutionary because former FIA president Max Mosley proposed a series of rules aiming for F1 to become an example of an economical and green sport (currently F1 cars consume up to 70L of fuel per 100 km). Plans include introducing completely free regulations in terms of engine capacity, configuration, type, and number of cylinders, but fuel quantity for the race is expected to be limited, so engines would need to become more efficient and powerful while their power should remain at the same level as today.
